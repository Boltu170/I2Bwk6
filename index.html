Defining an Organisation

People  +  Relationships  +  Common Purpose

Example: A Music Band

Multiple musicians + working relationships + the shared goal of producing music = an organisation.

Your college works the same way. So does Amazon. So does the NHS.

Buchanan and Huczynski (1991) define organisations as:

"Social arrangements for the controlled performance of collective goals."


An organisation is people brought together to carry out different roles. It works through:

• Inter-relationships — people connecting with each other

• Co-operation — working together toward the goal

• Sometimes conflict — disagreements are a normal part of every workplace


Think of an organisation as:

— A machine: every part must work together or the whole thing breaks down

— A living organism: it grows, adapts, and can become unhealthy

Source: Buchanan, D. and Huczynski, A. (1991) Organizational Behaviour. Prentice Hall.

The 5 Organisational Skills

Planning
Deciding what to do and how
Organising
Setting roles and resources up
Resourcing
Getting what is needed
Controlling
Checking it is working
People Management — looking after and motivating the team

Example: Running Glastonbury Festival

Plan the lineup  |  Organise 3,000 staff  |  Resource security, stages and infrastructure

Control crowds on the day  |  Manage 200,000 attendees and performers

Imagine needing to move a huge boulder. You would immediately face all five skills:

• Planning: How do we move it? What approach do we take?

• Organising: Who does what job?

• Resourcing: Do we use (a) brute strength, (b) a lever, or (c) a bulldozer?

• Controlling: Is it working? Is it safe?

• People managing: Keeping the team motivated and coordinated throughout


Choosing a bulldozer (technology) changes the entire structure of the task — fewer people are needed, different skills are required, and more planning is essential. Every business faces exactly this kind of decision.

Why Have a Structure?

A structure is a framework — without it, even large organisations collapse into confusion
Link individuals together
Group similar tasks
Give authority to act
Co-ordinate activities
Facilitate the smooth flow of work through the business

Example: Netflix

Netflix employs 12,000 people. Without a clear structure, nobody would know who commissions content, who manages technology, or who controls finance.

Structure means every person knows their role — and productions actually get completed on time.

Organisational Structure: The internal, formal framework of a business that shows how management is organised and linked together, and how authority is passed through the organisation.


An organisational chart makes clear:

• Who has overall responsibility for decision-making

• Formal relationships between people and departments

• The chain of command — how authority flows downward

• The span of control — how many people each manager supervises

• Communication channels — who officially communicates with whom

• Who each worker reports to

Source: Stimpson, P. and Farquharson, A. (2015) Cambridge International AS and A Level Business Coursebook. 3rd ed. Cambridge University Press.

5 Types of Organisational Structure

```
Functional — grouped by skill type (Finance, HR, Marketing as separate departments) Real world: Apple — distinct divisions for Hardware Engineering, Software, Retail, and Finance
Product / Service — each product line has its own dedicated team Real world: Procter and Gamble — separate divisions for Pampers, Gillette, Tide, and Ariel
Divisional — grouped by region, geography, or customer type Real world: HSBC — separate divisions for UK, Asia-Pacific, Americas, and Middle East
Matrix — employees belong to both a functional team AND a project team at the same time Real world: Deloitte — consultants report to a practice lead and also to a specific client project manager
Virtual / Network — electronically linked companies with a minimal central office Real world: Airbnb — a small HQ coordinates thousands of independent hosts worldwide

Functional: All workers with similar skills grouped together

  Advantage: Specialists share expertise and develop deep knowledge in their field

  Disadvantage: Departments can become isolated silos with poor cross-team communication


Product / Service: Each product line has its own complete team

  Advantage: Teams are highly focused on their specific product's success

  Disadvantage: Duplication of resources — each division needs its own HR, Finance, etc.


Divisional: Split by geography, product type, or customer group

  Advantage: Local knowledge and ability to respond to regional needs quickly

  Disadvantage: Head office may lose overall control and consistency


Matrix: Workers belong to BOTH a functional team AND a project team simultaneously

  Advantage: Flexible, encourages innovation and cross-department collaboration

  Disadvantage: Two managers can create conflicting priorities and confusion


Virtual / Network: Independent companies or individuals linked electronically, minimal HQ

  Advantage: Very flexible, low operating costs, access to global talent

  Disadvantage: Difficult to maintain consistent quality and build staff loyalty

Source: Stimpson and Farquharson (2015) Cambridge International A Level Business. Cambridge University Press. | ARU College Week 6 Lecture.

Hierarchy and Chain of Command

Hierarchy = levels of authority stacked from top to bottom of an organisation
Chain of Command = the route through which authority and instructions travel downward

Example: McDonald's Chain of Command

Global CEO — Regional Vice President — Country Manager — Area Manager — Store Manager — Shift Supervisor — Crew Member

Every instruction passes down that chain. A breakdown at any level causes delays across the whole operation.

Tall Structure
Many levels of hierarchy
Narrow span of control
Risk: slow communication
Flat Structure
Few levels of hierarchy
Wide span of control
Benefit: fast communication

A tall (narrow) structure has many levels of hierarchy. This creates three main problems:


1. Slow, distorted communication — messages pass through multiple managers and can arrive altered or delayed


2. Narrow spans of control — each manager oversees only a few people, creating unnecessary layers of management


3. Remoteness — staff at lower levels feel disconnected from decision-makers, reducing motivation and morale


Many modern organisations — including Spotify, Zappos and Haier — have actively flattened their structures to address exactly these problems.

Source: Stimpson and Farquharson (2015) Cambridge International A Level Business. Cambridge University Press.

Span of Control

Span of Control = the number of people who report directly to one manager
Wide Span (e.g. 8 people)

Benefit: More delegation
Benefit: Flatter structure overall
Risk: Manager stretched thin
Narrow Span (e.g. 3 people)

Benefit: Close supervision
Benefit: More support per worker
Risk: Creates taller structure

Example: A Premier League Football Club

Wide span: A head coach managing 25 squad players — must trust each one to perform their role independently

Narrow span: A specialist goalkeeping coach with just 3 players — able to monitor and develop each one closely

The ideal span of control depends on several factors:

• Task complexity — complex work requires closer supervision, so a narrower span is appropriate

• Worker skill level — experienced staff need less supervision, allowing a wider span

• Manager's experience — a confident, skilled manager can effectively handle more people

• Physical location — geographically spread teams are harder to supervise, requiring a narrower span

• Use of technology — digital monitoring tools allow managers to oversee larger teams today


There is no universally correct number. Context determines what works best in each situation.

Source: Stimpson and Farquharson (2015) Cambridge International A Level Business. Cambridge University Press. | ARU College Week 6 Lecture.

Centralised vs Decentralised

Centralised

All major decisions at the top
Head office controls everything
Benefit: consistent decisions
Risk: slow, staff feel powerless
Decentralised

Decisions made by local teams
Authority spread throughout
Benefit: fast, staff feel empowered
Risk: less consistency across sites

Example: McDonald's vs Zara

Centralised — McDonald's: The menu, pricing and branding are all decided centrally. Every outlet worldwide looks and operates identically.

Decentralised — Zara: Local store managers respond to regional trends independently. A new design can reach stores in just two weeks because teams act without waiting for head office.

Agile Organisation: A company whose structure, policies and capabilities allow employees to respond quickly to customer needs and changes in the business environment.


Agile organisations tend to be decentralised — decision-making power is given to the teams closest to the customer.


Google operates a partially decentralised model. Gmail was created by one engineer during a 20% personal project time policy — made possible because individual employees had the freedom to act without central approval for every idea.


Departmentalisation: The process of grouping people within an organisation according to function, division, matrix or network structure.

Source: Stimpson and Farquharson (2015) Cambridge International A Level Business. Cambridge University Press. | ARU College Week 6 Lecture.
